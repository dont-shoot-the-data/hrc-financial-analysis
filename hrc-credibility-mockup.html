Institutional Analysis

A financial review of the Human Rights Campaign, the organization that publishes the most widely cited data on fatal violence against transgender Americans. All figures sourced to IRS Form 990 filings via ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer.

Source Note: Every financial figure in this section is drawn from public IRS Form 990 filings for HRC Inc (EIN 52-1243457) and HRC Foundation (EIN 52-1481896), accessed through ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer and HRC's own financial reports page. Layoff reporting is sourced to The Advocate (Feb. 4, 2025). No claims are made about intent. The numbers are presented as filed. Program staff salaries, materials, and overhead are not captured in Schedule I individual grant figures; this analysis examines what reached individuals directly, not total programmatic spending.

A note on advocacy nonprofits

HRC would argue that advocacy organizations don't primarily give grants -- they hire staff who lobby, write reports, file lawsuits, and rate institutions. That's structurally true of the advocacy nonprofit model. The ACLU, NAACP, and similar organizations also spend heavily on staff and infrastructure.

The question is not whether advocacy nonprofits should employ staff. The question is whether an organization spending $9.9 million on consultants and $6.6 million on conferences is primarily doing advocacy work, or primarily sustaining an organization. Conferences alone were 4.2 times all external grants combined. Professional fees were 6.3 times all external grants. These are not the ratios of an organization maximizing impact. They are the ratios of an organization maximizing itself.

What the Balance Sheet Shows

The Foundation's FY2024 balance sheet (Part X, line 25) reports $7,440,551 in liabilities classified as "Due to Human Rights Campaign" -- money the Foundation owed to HRC Inc at year-end.

In addition, the Foundation made a $1,000,000 direct grant to HRC Inc for lobbying (Schedule I, Part II) -- the same amount as every other recent year.

Combined, the Foundation's financial obligations to HRC Inc ($8.4M) represented 30% of the Foundation's total expenses ($28.0M) that year.

Of the Foundation's $1,647,992 in grants to domestic organizations in FY2024, the single largest recipient was HRC Inc at $1,000,000. That leaves $647,992 in grants to all other domestic organizations combined. The next largest grant was $45,000 to the National Black Justice Coalition for polling research.

The Foundation's sole listed fundraising event in FY2024, "Chef's for Equality," generated $354,988 in gross receipts but cost $282,823 in direct expenses (venue $181K, food $48K, entertainment $2K, other $52K) -- a net loss of $87,235 after subtracting the $159,400 in contributions. 4. Trans-Specific Grants: The Six-Year Record

HRC Foundation's Schedule I, Part III discloses grants to domestic individuals by program category. This is where any trans-specific individual grant spending would appear.

What is the Transgender Justice Initiative (TJI)?

The Transgender Justice Initiative was HRC Foundation's dedicated program for direct support of transgender individuals. According to HRC's own 990 narratives, TJI led "economic empowerment programs, capacity-building initiatives, community engagement efforts, and public safety education campaigns that combat the discrimination faced by transgender people." It was the organizational home for small grants to trans individuals, the Elevate and Activate fellowship programs for trans women of color, community job fairs, and the annual National Justice & Advocacy Summit.

TJI is relevant to this analysis because it was HRC's primary vehicle for putting money directly into trans people's hands -- the one program where individual grants, measured in dollars reaching actual people, can be tracked through Schedule I filings. It is separate from HRC's broader programmatic work (the Corporate Equality Index, Welcoming Schools, the Fatal Violence report itself) where spending is absorbed into staff salaries and overhead and cannot be isolated on the 990.

The following data is extracted directly from each year's filing, covering every available fiscal year from FY2020 through FY2025:

FY2020 (Apr 2019 -- Mar 2020)
No TJI line item. The Transgender Justice Initiative is described in the Part III narrative as "an innovative new program" but had not yet produced individual grants. Schedule I lists only two categories: "Volunteer Travel Support" (125 recipients, $112,271) and "Fellow Stipends and Student Scholarships" (17 recipients, $14,900). Total individual grants: $127,171. Trans-specific individual grants: $0.
FY2021 (Apr 2020 -- Mar 2021)
The first TJI individual grants appear. Schedule I lists a single category: "Transgender Justice Initiative Grants": 32 recipients, $33,197. This is the only individual grant category listed this year (COVID-era budget; total Foundation expenses dropped to $13.8M). The Part III narrative describes the TJI small grants program distributing "$30,000 to 30 advocates" and the first National Justice & Advocacy Summit.
FY2022 (Apr 2021 -- Mar 2022)
TJI grants peak. Schedule I lists three categories. "Support for Participation in Transgender Justice Initiative Programs": 67 recipients, $75,181. "Support for Participation in HIV & Health Equity Programs": 16 recipients, $16,025. "Travel Support for Program Participation": 25 recipients, $16,765. Total individual grants: $107,971.
FY2023 (Apr 2022 -- Mar 2023)
The Transgender Justice Initiative line item disappears from Schedule I. Only two generic categories remain: "Travel Support for Program Participation" (130 recipients, $129,111) and "Stipends for Participation in HRCF Programs" (71 recipients, $64,202). Total individual grants: $193,313. The Schedule O narrative describes TJI's Lyft Rides grant program ($80K, routed through organizational partners) but no trans-specific individual grants are broken out. Trans-specific individual grants: $0.
FY2024 (Apr 2023 -- Mar 2024)
The trans line reappears, now merged with HIV & Health Equity into a single category: "HIV & Health Equity and Trans Justice Initiative Project Grants": 14 recipients, $62,915. The trans-only portion is not broken out. Alongside: Travel support (126 recipients, $215,993) and Stipends (83 recipients, $88,225). Total individual grants: $367,133. Schedule O describes TJI "distributing $75,000 in benefits to 55 trans and nonbinary organizations, individuals, and projects" -- but only $62,915 appears on the individual grants line.
FY2025 (Apr 2024 -- Mar 2025)
The same combined line item collapses 83% from its FY2024 level: "HIV & Health Equity and Trans Justice Initiative Project Grants": 11 recipients, $11,000 -- an average of $1,000 per person. Alongside: Travel support (118 recipients, $189,672) and Stipends (30 recipients, $52,250). Total individual grants: $252,922. Five months after this fiscal year ended, HRC eliminated all trans-focused programs entirely.

7. February 2025: Layoffs

On February 4, 2025, The Advocate reported that HRC cut approximately 20% of its workforce -- roughly 50 employees -- reducing staff from approximately 230 to 180. The cuts included:

Programs Eliminated

Complete elimination of trans-focused programs.

Complete elimination of youth programs.

Elimination of the internal DEI team leader position.

The elimination of trans-focused programs at HRC is documented by both The Advocate's reporting and HRC's own subsequent organizational statements. This occurred while HRC continued to position itself as the nation's primary tracker and advocate regarding fatal violence against transgender Americans.

8. Data Accuracy

This study's methodology involved independently verifying every case on HRC's annual reports of fatal violence against transgender people from 2015 through 2024 -- 304 cases -- against court records, booking photos, and local news coverage. The verification process documented recurring factual errors in HRC's published data, including misidentified suspect races, cases listed with incorrect jurisdictions, and individuals counted who did not meet the report's own stated inclusion criteria. These errors are documented case by case in the corresponding year files of this study.

These are not allegations. They are documented, verifiable discrepancies between what HRC published and what the underlying court and news records show.

The Question This Section Asks

HRC's annual reports on fatal violence against transgender people are cited by the White House, Congress, major news organizations, and academic researchers as authoritative data. Those reports form the basis of policy proposals, funding decisions, and public understanding of who is killing transgender Americans and why.

This section does not allege fraud or wrongdoing. It presents, from HRC's own IRS filings, how the organization that produces that data allocates its resources: 98 cents of every dollar stayed inside HRC in FY2025 -- paying salaries, consultants, conferences, travel, and organizational infrastructure. Of the 2% that did reach external recipients, $182,293 over six fiscal years can be traced to direct trans individual grants through the Foundation's named Transgender Justice Initiative. The Foundation's grants to its own lobbying arm ($5.6 million) over those same six years were 31 times the trans grants. The president's single-year compensation ($784,577) was 71 times the trans grants budget in FY2025. Then, in February 2025, every trans-focused program was eliminated.

The question is not whether HRC committed wrongdoing. The question is whether an organization that spent $407.6 million combined over six years while directing $182,293 in trans individual grants across that entire period, where 98% of spending sustains itself, should be treated as an authoritative, disinterested source on the subject it claims to lead.
